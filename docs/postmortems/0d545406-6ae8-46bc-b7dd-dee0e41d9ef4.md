## Root Cause Analysis

The `mock_inference` service experienced a severe degradation in its `p95 latency`, which escalated by approximately 15 times its normal baseline. While the direct cause of this latency spike within `mock_inference` is yet to be precisely determined, Sleuth's diagnostic hypothesis with 95% confidence points to a critical underlying issue: **the unreachability of the simulator service.**

The simulator is a foundational component within the Aegis ecosystem, vital for:
1.  **Log Correlation:** Aggregating and correlating logs across various services to pinpoint error sources.
2.  **Deployment Tracking:** Retrieving information about recent deployments, which is crucial for identifying changes that might have introduced regressions.
3.  **Contextual Diagnostics:** Providing the necessary environmental context for deeper problem analysis.

The `simulator_unreachable_error` signal directly correlated with the incident, confirming that this core diagnostic tool was non-functional. Consequently, Aegis was unable to perform the deeper analysis required to fully diagnose the `mock_inference` latency, effectively blocking automated incident resolution at a fundamental level. The simulator's unreachability transformed a service-specific anomaly into a systemic diagnostic failure.

## Preventative Guardrails

To prevent recurrence of similar diagnostic blocks and improve overall system resilience, the following preventative guardrails are suggested:

1.  **Robust Simulator Health Monitoring & Alerting:**
    *   Implement dedicated, high-priority monitoring for the simulator service's health, including liveness probes, readiness checks, and API latency/error rates.
    *   Configure immediate paging alerts for any degradation or unreachability of the simulator service itself, independent of other service anomalies.
    *   Establish dashboards providing real-time visibility into the simulator's operational status.

2.  **Enhanced Simulator Resilience & Redundancy:**
    *   **Architectural Review:** Conduct a thorough review of the simulator's architecture to identify single points of failure.
    *   **Multi-AZ Deployment:** Deploy the simulator across multiple availability zones to ensure continued operation during regional outages.
    *   **Automated Failover:** Implement automated failover mechanisms (e.g., active-passive or active-active) for simulator instances.
    *   **Capacity Planning:** Ensure the simulator is adequately provisioned to handle peak diagnostic loads without becoming a bottleneck.

3.  **Dependency-Aware Remediation & Escalation:**
    *   **Pre-emptive Actions:** If the simulator's health degrades, trigger automated alerts and potentially pre-emptive actions (e.g., scaling up resources, attempting restarts) *before* it impacts downstream diagnostic capabilities.
    *   **Fallback Diagnostic Pathways:** Explore developing limited diagnostic capabilities or access to critical data (e.g., recent deployment logs) that can function even when core services like the simulator are offline. This might involve local caches or direct, lower-level access.
    *   **Dedicated Incident Playbook:** Create a specific incident playbook for simulator outages, detailing immediate manual steps for restoration, escalation matrix, and guidelines for operating in a degraded diagnostic state.

4.  **Automated Self-Healing for Simulator:**
    *   Investigate and implement automated self-healing mechanisms for the simulator service, such as automatic restarts of unhealthy pods or instances, provided these actions can be performed safely without causing further disruption.
